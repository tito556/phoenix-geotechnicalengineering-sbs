A mid-rise mixed-use development broke ground near the Roosevelt Row Arts District last year, and the geotechnical report flagged something that surprised a few stakeholders: a lens of loose, saturated silty sand at about 18 feet below grade. Phoenix sits in a moderate seismic zone, and while the big faults are farther west, the basin effects here can amplify ground motion in ways that catch standard site classifications off guard. That project moved forward only after a detailed soil liquefaction analysis mapped the extent of the vulnerable layer and confirmed that the cyclic stress ratio stayed below the critical threshold under the design earthquake. It is the kind of scenario we see more often as infill development pushes into older floodplain deposits along the Salt River. When the water table is shallow and the fines content is just right, even Arizona soils deserve a hard look under seismic microzonation criteria before a single pile gets driven.

Liquefaction in Phoenix is not about proximity to the San Andreas—it is about basin resonance, shallow groundwater, and the fines content of the Salt River alluvium.

Methodology and scope

The field crew typically arrives with an AWD drilling rig fitted for standard penetration testing, because the correlation between SPT blow counts and liquefaction resistance is still the backbone of most simplified procedures in practice. We log the stratigraphy continuously, pull undisturbed samples with a pitcher barrel where the silts get tricky, and measure groundwater depth with a dedicated piezometer—no shortcuts, because a half-foot error in the water table changes the factor of safety. Back in the lab, sieve stacks get shaken down to the No. 200 according to ASTM D2487, and Atterberg limits are run on the fines fraction to distinguish plastic clays from non-plastic silts that behave completely differently under cyclic loading. For sites where the client needs a higher-resolution profile, we pair the SPT data with a CPT test pushed through the suspect zone; the cone tip resistance and sleeve friction give us a nearly continuous log of soil behavior type that tightens up the liquefaction potential index significantly.

Local ground factors

ASCE 7-22 Chapter 21 and the IBC require a site-specific liquefaction evaluation for Seismic Design Categories D, E, and F when susceptible soils are present and the water table is within 50 feet of the ground surface. In Phoenix, much of the downtown core and the industrial corridors along the I-10 fall into Site Class D by default, but the presence of Holocene-age alluvium with interbedded silts and sands can push a site into Class E or even F if the geotechnical investigation is not thorough. Skipping the analysis—or running it with generic assumptions about groundwater depth—opens the door to differential settlement, lateral spreading, and bearing capacity failure during a moderate event on the McDowell or Cave Creek fault zones. The cost of a proper deep excavation support design or a ground improvement program after the fact dwarfs the investment in getting the liquefaction assessment right from the start.

Questions and answers

Is liquefaction really a concern in Phoenix, given the distance from the California plate boundary?

Yes, and it is a common misconception that distance from the San Andreas eliminates the hazard. Phoenix is within the Basin and Range tectonic province, with active faults such as the McDowell and Cave Creek systems capable of generating moderate earthquakes. More importantly, the deep sedimentary basin beneath the Salt River Valley can amplify ground shaking at periods that affect mid-rise structures. Combined with shallow groundwater in many parts of the city and loose alluvial deposits, the conditions for liquefaction are met more often than people expect.

What is the typical cost range for a liquefaction analysis on a commercial site in Phoenix?

A site-specific liquefaction evaluation—including field drilling, SPT sampling, laboratory testing for fines content and Atterberg limits, and the engineering analysis report—typically runs between US$2.150 and US$3.880, depending on the number of borings and the depth of the liquefiable layers. The final scope is always tailored to the project size and the complexity of the subsurface profile.

How many borings are needed for a proper liquefaction assessment?

There is no fixed number—it depends on the site footprint and the variability of the subsurface. For a typical commercial lot of one to two acres, we usually recommend a minimum of two borings extending at least 50 feet deep or to bedrock, whichever is shallower. If the stratigraphy is highly variable, as is common near the historic Salt River channels, we add intermediate CPT soundings to capture lateral changes in soil behavior type without increasing the boring count unnecessarily.

What happens if liquefaction potential is found? Does that kill the project?

Not at all. Finding liquefiable soils does not stop a project—it simply defines the engineering path forward. Depending on the depth and thickness of the critical layer, the factor of safety, and the structural loads, mitigation can range from shallow foundation alternatives like a mat foundation that tolerates some settlement, to ground improvement with stone columns or vibrocompaction that densifies the deposit before construction. The analysis gives the design team the numbers they need to choose the right approach.
